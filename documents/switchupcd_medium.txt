7 Tips to Help You Survive While Homeless

I have experienced many types of homelessness, including street homeless (sleeping on the ground), car homeless (sleeping in a car), hotel homeless, and shelter homeless. These are seven tips from direct experience.

1. Don't Look Homeless

When you look homeless, you will be treated like the stereotypical homeless person, which can cost you job opportunities.

Studies show that more than half of unhoused people have jobs. But having a job doesn't give you access to a shower.

When showers aren't an option, get a long-lasting hairstyle and use baby wipes. Baby wipes can be used anywhere for cleaning skin and are stored in a backpack easily.

Looking like you belong will get you places you never thought you could be.

2. Sleep

Find a place to sleep. You need quality sleep to recover your physical and mental health.

Most shelters get a bad rap for good reason. Use earplugs to block out noise and get better rest in a shelter environment.

3. Get Storage

You can't sleep in a storage unit, but you can use one to get yourself situated or make your day-to-day life easier.

Being limited by the space a single backpack provides is difficult when you are homeless. You spend too much time swapping things in and out of that bag.

Buying items by the unit instead of in bulk wastes money when you lack storage space.

Consider these factors when renting a storage unit:
- Proximity to your shelter and area of operations
- Allowed time per check-in
- Temperature control and weather insulation

With a storage unit, holding onto valuable items such as laptops for online work is much safer.

4. Get More Storage

One or two backpacks to sort your stuff is always great. Consider organizing your belongings across multiple bags:

- Essentials you carry by default
- Clothes you can swap in and out to stay fresh
- Blankets for your shelter
- Bathroom tools and supplies
- Medical and cleaning supplies
- Technology for work
- Miscellaneous work items

A toiletry bag is essential for organizing bathroom supplies. Hanging toiletry bags are especially practical in shelter environments.

You're homeless, not broke. Being miserable for three months to save up for one month's rent is not worth it when you can make being homeless manageable.

5. Building Rotation

When you have downtime, you need places to go. Libraries aren't open 24/7, and some shelters kick you out during the daytime. That's why you need a building rotation for every day of the week.

Good options:
- Libraries (free, climate controlled, WiFi access)
- Coffee shops
- Parks
- Gyms (access to showers)

These places let you spend hours on your phone or laptop. That's more time you have to get back on your feet.

6. Mobile Data

Using the WiFi of others isn't reliable. Your best bet is a phone with mobile data. To get a phone without a physical address, see Tip 7 below.

7. Legal Workarounds

Legal documents are required in many scenarios: ID, physical address, phone number, and email address.

Email: Many email providers let you create an email address for free.

Phone number options:
- Purchase a plan from a carrier
- Purchase a SIM card and activate a prepaid plan
- Use a VoIP service such as Google Voice
- Use an XMPP service such as JMP.chat

Physical address: You can use a homeless shelter as your physical address. Mail forwarding services can also provide a physical address for legal purposes.

ID: In the United States, non-driver identification cards can be registered as long as you have a legal address and a supporting document such as a birth certificate. Check your state's licensing office for specific requirements.

Bonus: Food Resources

If you need food, you have more options than you may think:
- Food banks
- Religious organizations
- Public events
- Fast food mobile app signup rewards (many chains offer free items for new app users)
- Ask people directly — most are willing to help
